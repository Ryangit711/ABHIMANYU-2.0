# How to Become Canadian (in the eyes of employers)

**Philosophy:** Canada isn't a place you move to. It's a place you become. The goal: when a hiring manager reads your resume, they think "Canadian professional" — not "international candidate."

---

## The 6 Pillars

### 1. Education (DONE)
- KPU Post-Baccalaureate Diploma in Technical Management & Services (2023-2025)
- This is your Canadian anchor. It's on your resume. It's real.
- **Action:** Already done. Nothing more needed.

### 2. Network (START THIS WEEK)
- Join Canadian associations
- Connect with KPU alumni
- Attend Canadian events
- **Action:** Join PMI Vancouver, CMA, Small Business BC this week.

### 3. Language
- Stop thinking in US terms. Think in Canadian terms.
- "Healthcare" in Canada ≠ "healthcare" in the US. Learn the difference.
- Canadian business culture is more consensus-driven than US.
- **Action:** Read Canadian business news (Globe and Mail, Financial Post). Absorb the language.

### 4. References (GET 2-3 CANADIAN)
- You need Canadian references. Not US ones.
- KPU professors. People you've done business with here.
- **Action:** Email 2-3 KPU professors. Ask if they'd be a reference.

**Email template:**
```
Subject: Reference Request — Aman Kumar

Hi Professor [Name],

I hope you're doing well. I wanted to reach out because I'm 
actively job searching in operations/strategy here in Vancouver, 
and I was wondering if you'd be comfortable being a reference 
for me.

I really valued your course on [topic] and the feedback you 
gave on my [project/paper]. If you're open to it, I'd be happy 
to share my resume and a brief summary of my background so you 
have context.

No pressure at all — I completely understand if you're not able 
to. Thank you for considering it.

Best regards,
Aman Kumar
```

### 5. Content (WRITE ABOUT CANADIAN BUSINESS)
- Write LinkedIn posts about Canadian operations
- Show you understand the Canadian market
- **Action:** Write 1 LinkedIn post per week about Canadian business.

**Example post:**
```
One thing I've noticed about Canadian business culture since 
relocating here:

The consensus-driven approach is real. In my experience, decisions 
take longer but implementation is smoother because everyone is 
already aligned.

In the US, I'd see CEO-mandated changes rolled out top-down. In 
Canada, I see more collaborative decision-making — slower to start, 
faster to execute.

Both approaches work. But understanding the difference matters 
if you're building operations here.
```

### 6. Volunteer (BOARD POSITIONS)
- Non-profit board positions = Canadian experience
- Advisory roles at startups = Canadian network
- **Action:** Search "board opportunity Vancouver" on Volunteer Vancouver.

## The Philosophy (spoon-feed)

You're not trying to "become Canadian" by pretending to be something you're not. You're trying to show Canadian employers that:

1. You're here. (KPU diploma, Vancouver address)
2. You're staying. (PR filing, long-term commitment)
3. You understand the context. (Canadian business news, local associations)
4. You have local connections. (KPU alumni, Canadian references)
5. You think like a Canadian. (Consensus-driven, collaborative)

The goal isn't to erase your past. It's to show that your past + your Canadian present = a complete professional who happens to have international experience.

**Canadian is not a replacement for your experience. It's a complement to it.**
